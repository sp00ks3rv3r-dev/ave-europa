# Part 1 — Analytical (the long-form register)

## I · Unification — "One Hand on the Brake"
*A single national veto can freeze the whole Union. Federation is not a dream; it is the end of paralysis.*

Europe does not lack power. It lacks the ability to use it. On foreign policy, defence and migration, the Union still moves by unanimity, which means any one capital can halt twenty-six others. This is not prudence. It is paralysis dressed up as respect for sovereignty.

The result is a continent that debates while history is decided elsewhere. Sanctions are watered down to the price of the most reluctant member. Common positions arrive after the moment that needed them has passed. A Union that can be stopped by one hand on the brake cannot lead, cannot defend, and will not be taken seriously.

The answer is not a superstate that swallows its nations. It is a federation with the discipline to leave to each people what belongs to it, and the strength to decide together what must be decided together. Qualified-majority voting on foreign and security policy. A Commission president the citizens actually elect. A written constitution that says plainly where power sits and who answers for it.

Critics call this overreach. The truth is the opposite. A Union that cannot act is the one that loses sovereignty, because the decisions still get made, only by Washington, Beijing or Moscow instead of by Europeans. Federation is how twenty-seven half-voices become one the world has to hear.

Europe is not too united. It is united in name and divided in every moment that counts. Ending the veto is where the repair begins.

## II · Reindustrialisation — "Build, Power, Bank"
*A Europe that cannot make its own chips, generate its own power, or clear its own payments is a Europe that takes orders.*

Sovereignty is not a flag. It is a supply chain. The hard question is simple: can Europe build, power and bank for itself? Today the honest answer is no, and every dependence is a lever in someone else's hand.

The most advanced chips are made in Taiwan. The cloud that runs European business is largely American. The energy that should be abundant at home is bought abroad at a premium. We wrote the world's rules on data and competition while losing the capacity to make the things those rules govern. Regulation is not a substitute for production.

Reindustrialisation is the correction. Reshore the critical sectors: semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, defence components, the materials everything else depends on. Finish the single market that is still half-built in services, capital and energy. Back European champions instead of apologising for them. Treat technological independence in AI, chips and space as a security requirement, not an industrial hobby.

This costs money and political nerve, and it collides with the comfortable habit of importing what we used to make. But a continent that offshores its capabilities also offshores its choices. When the factory is somewhere else, so is the decision.

Europe invented the modern industrial world. It can build in it again. The alternative is to keep writing elegant rules for an economy it no longer controls.

## III · Remilitarisation — "An Army, Not an Alliance"
*Coordination is not deterrence. Europe will be safe when its defence belongs to no one but Europe.*

In 2022 Europe watched a full-scale war return to its continent and discovered that its security still ran through Washington. The weapons, the intelligence, the command, even the decision to act, all depended on a partner who is entitled, one day, to look elsewhere. An ally is a blessing. A dependence is a vulnerability.

Twenty-seven national armies that cannot easily fight together are not a defence; they are a duplication. Europe spends enormous sums for fragmented capability, incompatible systems, and a deterrent that, for most members, is borrowed. Coordination committees do not stop tanks.

The honest answer is a single joint European army under unified command, built on the forces that already exist, with a sovereign deterrent that answers to Europe alone. Not to replace national pride in national regiments, but to give them a spine that holds when it is tested.

This is not hostility to America, nor an invitation to Moscow or Beijing. It is independence from all of them. Sovereignty means owing our defence to no one. A Europe that cannot guarantee its own borders has already conceded the most basic question a polity must answer: can it protect its people.

We are not asking to become a garrison. We are asking to stop being a protectorate.

## IV · Repatriation & Borders — "A Border Is a Promise"
*Free movement inside Europe depends on real control at its edge. Order is the precondition for openness, not its enemy.*

Open borders inside Europe were one of the great achievements of the project. They are also impossible to sustain without firm borders around it. A house with no outer door cannot keep its inner doors open. The promise of free movement rests, whether we admit it or not, on the assumption that the external frontier is controlled. For years it has not been.

A serious migration policy begins by separating the questions that demagogues blur on purpose. Asylum for those genuinely owed it is a duty and a point of honour. Uncontrolled, irregular mass entry is a different matter, and pretending otherwise has poisoned the debate and handed it to the worst voices on every side.

The pragmatic answer is unglamorous: a genuinely guarded external border, decisions made quickly and upstream rather than after dangerous crossings, and the consistent return of those with no right to stay. Paired with it, an honest commitment to integration, to the European languages, laws and civic norms that turn arrival into belonging.

This is not about who Europeans are by blood. It is about what living here asks of anyone who comes: to join a common project, not merely to occupy a space within it. A continent that cannot say who enters cannot promise anything to those already here. Control is what makes generosity sustainable.

## V · Civilisation & Continuity — "What We Inherit, We Owe"
*A civilisation that forgets where it came from cannot decide where it is going. Continuity is not nostalgia; it is direction.*

Europe is not a market with nice cathedrals attached. It is a civilisation: the myth and courage of the ancient world, the reason of Greece, the law of Rome, the conscience of Christendom, the fire of the Enlightenment, the discipline of secular democracy. Strip that inheritance out and what remains is an administrative zone with a currency, easy to manage and impossible to love.

To say so is not to build a museum or to rank peoples. European identity is layered, not singular. You are of your region, your nation and your continent at once, and none of these cancels the others. A Breton, a Bavarian and a Pole lose nothing by also being Europeans; they gain a wider name for what they already are.

The danger runs in two directions. One side would freeze culture into a relic and bolt the door. The other treats heritage as an embarrassment to be apologised away. Both end in the same place: a Europe that cannot tell newcomers, or its own children, what it is they are joining.

Continuity is the alternative. Keep the languages alive. Teach the history honestly, including its crimes. Expect those who settle here to enter the common civic life rather than live beside it. We did not invent this inheritance. We hold it in trust, and we owe it forward.

## VI · Environmental Realism — "Clean and Strong, Not Poor and Pure"
*A green Europe that deindustrialises has not saved the planet. It has only exported its emissions and its jobs.*

No serious European doubts that the climate must be addressed. The argument is about method, and on method the prevailing approach has failed on its own terms. A policy that shutters European industry simply moves the smokestacks to China and the dependence to everyone, while telling citizens the higher bills are a virtue.

That is not environmentalism. It is the appearance of it, bought with industrial decline. A continent that grows poorer and weaker in the name of the climate will not be imitated; it will be pitied and overtaken. Virtue that cannot be afforded does not spread.

Environmental realism starts with the energy source the data, not the slogans, support: nuclear. Abundant, dispatchable, low-carbon, and ours to build. Pair it with renewables where they make sense, a serious grid that crosses borders, and an honest accounting of what each policy actually costs in jobs and in sovereignty.

The goal is a Europe that is clean and strong, not poor and pure. Decarbonisation should be a demonstration of European capability, the kind of thing this civilisation has always done best, not a penance it performs while its competitors laugh and build coal.

We can power ourselves and protect the climate at once. We cannot do either by managing our own decline.

# Part 2 — Accessible (the youth / non-policy register)

## I · "How One Country Can Freeze All of Europe"

Imagine 27 friends trying to pick a restaurant, with a rule that everyone has to agree. One person says no, and nobody eats. That is roughly how Europe makes some of its biggest decisions, like foreign policy and defence: one country can block all the others.

It sounds fair. In practice it means Europe is slow exactly when the world is fast. While leaders wait for the last holdout, the moment passes, and the big calls about Europe end up getting made in Washington or Beijing instead.

The fix isn't a giant super-government that erases your country. It's simpler: for the things Europe has to do together, let a strong majority decide instead of letting one hand pull the brake. Your country keeps what's local. Europe gets the ability to actually act.

Slow isn't safe. Slow is how you get left behind.

## II · "Why Europe Can't Make Its Own Phone Chips"

The most advanced chips in your phone aren't made in Europe. They're made in Taiwan. The cloud that stores your photos is mostly American. A lot of the energy Europe runs on is bought from somewhere else.

Here's why that matters to you: whoever makes the thing also gets to set the terms. If a factory on the other side of the world has a bad week, your prices, your apps, even your job can feel it. Writing clever rules about technology doesn't help much if you can't actually build the technology.

So the idea is straightforward. Make more of the essential stuff here again: chips, medicine, energy, the basics. Not everything, but the things you can't afford to beg for.

A Europe that can build, power and pay for itself gets to make its own choices. One that can't, takes orders.

## III · "Why Europe Still Waits for America to Defend It"

When war came back to Europe in 2022, something became obvious: Europe couldn't really defend itself on its own. The weapons, the intelligence, even a lot of the big decisions still ran through the United States.

America has been a good ally. But relying on someone else for your safety is a bet that they'll never be looking the other way. And 27 separate national armies that can't easily work together aren't as strong as they sound.

The idea isn't to ditch your country's soldiers or pick a fight with anyone. It's to make Europe's defences actually work together, so the continent can protect its own borders without asking permission first.

Being safe shouldn't depend on a friend staying interested. A Europe that can defend itself owes its safety to no one.

## IV · "Why Borders and Fairness Go Together"

Inside Europe you can travel from one country to the next without stopping. That freedom is amazing, and it only works because Europe controls the border around the outside. A house can keep its inside doors open because the front door has a lock.

There are two different questions that often get mixed up on purpose. Helping people who are genuinely in danger is something a decent society does. Letting in anyone, with no system and no checks, is a different thing, and pretending they're the same has made the whole conversation toxic.

A calm approach: actually guard the outer border, decide cases quickly and fairly, return people who have no right to stay, and seriously help those who do stay to settle in, learn the language, and join the life here.

You can be welcoming and organised at the same time. In fact, you have to be.

## V · "Why Your Grandparents' Language Still Matters"

You can be from your town, your country and Europe all at once. None of those cancel each other out. A kid in Lisbon and a kid in Warsaw are very different and also, obviously, both European. That layering is the whole point, not a problem to fix.

Europe isn't just a market with old churches in the background. It's a long story: Greek ideas, Roman law, centuries of art, science, faith and argument. That story is why this place feels like this place.

Caring about that doesn't mean freezing everything in a museum, or looking down on anyone. It means keeping the languages alive, learning the real history including the ugly parts, and inviting people who move here to actually join in, not just live nearby.

You inherited something huge. The point isn't to guard it nervously. It's to keep it alive and pass it on.

## VI · "Why Nuclear Might Be the Green Choice"

A lot of people hear "nuclear" and think danger. But if the goal is loads of clean electricity that keeps working even when the wind drops and the sun sets, nuclear is one of the best tools we have. Low carbon, reliable, and something Europe can build for itself.

Here's the trap to avoid. "Going green" by shutting down European factories doesn't save the planet. It just moves the pollution to China and sends the jobs along with it, while your energy bill climbs. That isn't clean. It's just poorer.

The smarter version: build nuclear, add wind and solar where they fit, connect the grids across countries, and be honest about what each choice really costs.

The aim is a Europe that's clean and strong, not poor and pure. You shouldn't have to choose between a good climate and a good life.
