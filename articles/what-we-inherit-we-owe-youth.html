Why your grandparents' language still matters

You can belong to your town, your country and Europe all at once. None of them cancel the others out.

Vigilia·Plain edition·19 June 2026·1 min read

You can be from your town, from your country, and from Europe, all at the same time. None of those cancel each other out. A kid in Lisbon and a kid in Warsaw can be wildly different and also, obviously, both European. That layering isn't a problem to fix; it's kind of the whole point.

Europe isn't just a shared market with some pretty old churches in the background. It's a long, messy, brilliant story: Greek ideas, Roman law, centuries of art and faith and argument, and a stubborn habit of reinventing itself. That story is the reason this place feels like this place and not somewhere else.

Caring about that doesn't mean freezing everything in a museum, and it definitely doesn't mean looking down on anyone. It means keeping the languages alive, learning the real history including the ugly chapters, and inviting people who move here to actually join in, not just live next door.

Why does it matter to you? Because if a place can't say what it is, it can't really offer you anything to belong to. Identity isn't a cage. It's the thing that gives "us" a meaning worth sharing.

You inherited something huge. The point was never to guard it nervously behind glass. It's to keep it alive, and hand it on.
