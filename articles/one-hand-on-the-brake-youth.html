I · Unification

How one country can freeze the whole of Europe

One stubborn government can stop the other twenty-six. Here's why that quietly decides your future.

Vigilia·Plain edition·19 June 2026·1 min read

Imagine twenty-seven friends trying to pick where to eat, with one rule: everyone has to agree. One person holds out, and nobody eats. That is roughly how Europe makes some of its biggest decisions, like foreign policy, defence and migration. Any single country can block all the others.

It sounds fair, even polite. In practice it means Europe is slow exactly when the world is moving fast. While leaders wait for the last holdout to come around, the moment passes. Sanctions get watered down to whatever the most reluctant government will accept. And the big calls about Europe's future end up getting made somewhere else: in Washington, in Beijing, in Moscow.

Here's the part that matters for you. When Europe can't act together, it doesn't keep its independence. It just hands the decision to someone bigger. "Sovereignty" that can't be used isn't sovereignty; it's a nice word printed on a stalled machine.

The fix isn't some giant super-state that erases your country. It's simpler than that. For the handful of things Europe genuinely has to do together, let a strong majority decide, instead of letting one hand pull the emergency brake. Your country keeps what's local and personal. Europe gets the ability to actually move.

Slow isn't safe. In a fast world, slow is just a quiet way of losing.
