III · Remilitarisation

Why Europe still waits for America to defend it

When war came back in 2022, Europe found out its safety wasn't really in its own hands.

Vigilia·Plain edition·19 June 2026·1 min read

When a full-scale war returned to Europe in 2022, something uncomfortable became obvious: Europe couldn't really defend itself on its own. The weapons, the intelligence, even a lot of the big decisions still ran through the United States.

America has been a genuine ally. But building your safety on someone else's choices is a bet: the bet that they'll never, ever be looking the other way. People and politics change. A friend today can be distracted, broke, or busy tomorrow. And here's the quieter problem: Europe has twenty-seven separate national armies that can't easily work together. That's not as strong as it sounds; it's a lot of duplication and not much punch.

The idea isn't to ditch your country's soldiers or to go pick a fight with anyone. It's to make Europe's defences actually fit together, built on the forces that already exist, so the continent can protect its own borders without first asking permission.

What does this mean for you? Just the most basic thing a place owes the people who live in it: the ability to keep them safe without depending on a favour from abroad.

Being safe shouldn't depend on a faraway friend staying interested. A Europe that can defend itself owes its safety to no one.
