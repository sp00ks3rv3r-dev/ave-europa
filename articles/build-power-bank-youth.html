Why Europe can't make its own phone chips

The most advanced tech you use isn't built here. That's not a tech problem; it's a power problem.

Vigilia·Plain edition·19 June 2026·1 min read

The most advanced chips inside your phone aren't made in Europe. They're made in Taiwan. The cloud that stores your photos and runs your favourite apps is mostly American. A big chunk of the energy Europe runs on is bought from somewhere else.

Why should you care? Because whoever makes the thing also gets to set the terms. If a single factory on the other side of the world has a bad month, your prices go up, your apps glitch, and jobs here feel it. Europe spent years writing clever rules about technology, which is fine, except you can't regulate something you can't build. A referee with no team of his own never wins the match.

So the idea is refreshingly simple: make more of the essential stuff here again. Not everything. Just the things you really can't afford to beg for: chips, medicine, energy, the basics that everything else sits on.

This isn't cheap and it isn't quick. It means backing European companies instead of apologising for them, and finishing a single market that's still half-built. But a continent that makes its own essentials gets to make its own choices.

A Europe that can build, power and pay for itself sets its own terms. One that can't, takes orders. It's that blunt.
