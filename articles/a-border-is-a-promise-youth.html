IV · Borders

Why open borders need a strong front door

Travelling Europe without stopping at borders is amazing. It only works if the outer edge is actually controlled.

Vigilia·Plain edition·19 June 2026·1 min read

One of the best things about Europe is that you can travel from one country to the next without ever stopping at a border. That freedom is real, and most people your age have never known anything else. But it only works because of something less visible: the border around the outside of Europe. A house can leave its inside doors open precisely because the front door has a lock.

Here's where the conversation usually goes wrong, often on purpose. Two completely different questions get blended into one. Helping people who are genuinely in danger is something a decent society does, full stop. Letting anyone in, with no system and no checks, is a separate question entirely. Pretending they're the same thing has made the whole debate toxic and handed it to the angriest voices on every side.

A calmer approach is honestly pretty boring: actually guard the outer border, decide cases quickly and fairly instead of after dangerous crossings, return people who have no right to stay, and seriously help the people who do stay to settle in, learn the language, and join the life here.

None of that is about who counts as European by blood. It's about what living somewhere asks of you: to join in, not just to occupy a spot.

You can be welcoming and organised at the same time. In fact, that's the only version that lasts.
